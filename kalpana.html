Kalpana

Kalpana Chawla was the first Indian-origin woman to travel to space, inspiring millions with her achievements in NASA.

Kalpana Chawla was an American astronaut of Indian origin and the first woman of Indian descent to go to space. She was born in Karnal, Haryana, India on March 17, 1962. She became a naturalized US citizen after marrying flight instructor Jean-Pierre Harrison. She first flew on the Space Shuttle Columbia in 1997 and was part of the crew on the ill-fated STS-107 mission in 2003, where the Columbia disintegrated upon reentry, killing all seven astronauts, including Chawla.
Kalpana Chawla was fascinated with flying from a young age and was inspired by India's first astronaut, Rakesh Sharma. She graduated from Tagore School in Karnal and then pursued a Bachelor of Science degree in Aeronautical Engineering from Punjab Engineering College, Chandigarh. She was the first woman to earn a degree in aeronautical engineering from the college. She later earned a Master of Science degree in aerospace engineering from the University of Texas at Arlington and a Doctor of Philosophy degree in aerospace engineering from the University of Colorado. Career at NASA: Chawla joined NASA in 1988 as a research scientist, working on complex air flow simulations. She was selected as an astronaut candidate in 1995. She flew on two Space Shuttle missions: STS-87 (1997): Chawla served as a mission specialist and robotic arm operator, spending over 15 days in space. STS-107 (2003): She was a mission specialist on this research mission, which focused on conducting approximately 80 experiments. The STS-107 mission ended tragically when the Space Shuttle Columbia broke apart during reentry, killing all seven astronauts on board. Legacy: Kalpana Chawla is remembered as a pioneer and inspiration for women and aspiring scientists and engineers, particularly those of Indian descent. Several awards and scholarships have been established in her name. A commercial cargo spacecraft and several hills on Mars are named in her honor.
